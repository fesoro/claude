## Text 1 — Remote Work: The New Normal

Working from home was once considered a luxury — something only freelancers and a few lucky employees could enjoy. But after 2020, remote work became the standard for millions of people around the world, especially in the technology industry.

**The Benefits**

For many workers, the advantages of remote work are clear. There is no commute, which saves both time and money. A person who used to spend an hour each way getting to the office now has two extra hours per day. That is ten hours a week — or more than five hundred hours a year. Many people use this time for exercise, hobbies, or simply sleeping more.

Remote workers also report higher levels of productivity. Without the distractions of a busy office — colleagues stopping by your desk, loud conversations, unnecessary meetings — many people find they can concentrate better and get more done in less time.

**The Challenges**

However, remote work is not perfect. One of the biggest challenges is isolation. When you work from home every day, you miss the social interaction that comes naturally in an office. Small things like having lunch with a colleague or chatting by the coffee machine might seem unimportant, but they help build relationships and a sense of belonging.

Another common problem is the difficulty of separating work from personal life. When your office is your bedroom or living room, it can be hard to "switch off" at the end of the day. Research shows that remote workers tend to work longer hours than their office-based counterparts, even though they feel more productive.

Communication can also suffer. In an office, you can walk over to someone's desk and ask a quick question. Remotely, you need to send a message and wait for a reply. Important context can be lost in text-based communication, leading to misunderstandings.

**The Hybrid Model**

Many companies have found a middle ground: the hybrid model. Employees work from home two or three days a week and come to the office for the rest. This approach tries to combine the flexibility of remote work with the social and collaborative benefits of being in the same physical space.

Whether fully remote, hybrid, or in-office, one thing is clear: the way we think about work has changed permanently. The traditional nine-to-five, five-days-a-week office routine is no longer the only option.

## Text 2 — How to Prepare for a Career Change

Changing careers can feel overwhelming, especially if you have been in the same field for several years. However, with the right preparation, it is absolutely possible to make a successful transition. Here are some practical steps.

**Step 1: Assess Your Transferable Skills**

Before you start applying for jobs in a new field, take time to identify your transferable skills — abilities that are valuable in any industry. These include communication, problem-solving, project management, teamwork, and time management. Even if your job title changes completely, many of the skills you have developed are still relevant.

**Step 2: Research the New Field**

Talk to people who already work in the field you are interested in. LinkedIn is an excellent tool for this. Send polite messages to professionals and ask if they would be willing to have a short chat about their experience. Most people are happy to help. Also, read industry blogs, listen to podcasts, and follow relevant companies on social media.

**Step 3: Fill the Gaps**

If the new field requires skills or knowledge you do not have yet, invest in learning. Online platforms like Coursera, Udemy, and freeCodeCamp offer affordable courses. You can also contribute to open-source projects, build a portfolio, or earn certifications. The key is to show that you are serious about the transition and that you are actively learning.

**Step 4: Update Your CV and Online Presence**

Your CV should highlight your transferable skills and any new skills you have acquired. Tailor it for the new industry — do not just list your old job responsibilities. Update your LinkedIn profile to reflect your new direction. If relevant, create a portfolio or personal website to showcase your work.

**Step 5: Start Small**

You do not have to make the switch overnight. Consider freelancing, volunteering, or taking on a part-time project in the new field before committing fully. This gives you real experience to put on your CV and helps you confirm that the new career is the right fit for you.

## Text 3 — Workplace Culture: What to Expect at International Companies

If you are preparing to work at an international company, understanding workplace culture is just as important as having the right technical skills. Here are some common aspects of workplace culture at global tech companies.

**Direct Communication**

In many Western companies, people are expected to communicate directly and clearly. If you disagree with an idea in a meeting, it is acceptable — and often encouraged — to say so respectfully. Phrases like "I see your point, but I think..." or "I'd like to suggest an alternative..." are common. This can feel uncomfortable if you come from a culture where disagreement is expressed indirectly.

**Flat Hierarchies**

Many tech companies have relatively flat organisational structures. This means there are fewer levels of management, and junior employees are encouraged to share their ideas and opinions. Your manager might ask you to call them by their first name rather than using formal titles.

**Feedback Culture**

Regular feedback is a normal part of working at international companies. You will receive feedback on your work — both positive and constructive — and you will also be expected to give feedback to others, including your manager. This is not criticism; it is seen as a way to help everyone improve.

**Work-Life Balance**

Most international companies value work-life balance. Working excessive overtime is generally not seen as a sign of dedication — it may actually be seen as a sign of poor time management. It is usually acceptable to leave the office on time, take your full lunch break, and use all your holiday days.

**Meetings and Time**

Punctuality is important. If a meeting starts at 10:00, you should be there at 10:00 — or even a few minutes early. Meetings usually have a clear agenda, and everyone is expected to come prepared. If you need to miss a meeting, let the organiser know in advance.
