## Text

Climate change is arguably the most pressing issue facing humanity today. The scientific consensus is clear: global temperatures are rising due to human activities, particularly the burning of fossil fuels and deforestation. If left unaddressed, the consequences could be catastrophic — rising sea levels, extreme weather events, and the collapse of ecosystems that support all life on Earth.

**The Evidence**

The Intergovernmental Panel on Climate Change (IPCC) has warned that global temperatures have already risen by approximately 1.1°C above pre-industrial levels. The organisation projects that unless significant action is taken, temperatures could rise by 1.5 to 4°C by the end of this century. Each additional degree of warming increases the frequency and severity of heatwaves, floods, droughts, and storms.

**Individual vs Collective Responsibility**

A common debate in environmental discourse concerns where responsibility lies. Some argue that individuals must change their behaviour — flying less, reducing meat consumption, and adopting renewable energy sources in their homes. Others contend that individual actions, while valuable, are insufficient without systemic change. According to this view, governments and corporations, which are responsible for the vast majority of global emissions, must take the lead.

**What Can Be Done?**

Encouragingly, solutions do exist. Renewable energy — particularly solar and wind power — has become increasingly affordable and widespread. Electric vehicles are replacing petrol-powered cars in many countries. Reforestation projects are helping to absorb carbon dioxide from the atmosphere. International agreements, such as the Paris Agreement, aim to coordinate global efforts to limit warming.

**Conclusion**

The challenge of climate change is immense, but it is not insurmountable. What is required is political will, international cooperation, and a genuine shift in both individual behaviour and corporate practice. The decisions made in the coming decades will determine the kind of world future generations inherit.
