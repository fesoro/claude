## Story 1: The Time I Failed a Technical Interview

A few years ago, I applied for a senior backend role at a company I really wanted to work for. I was confident going in — I had seven years of experience, a strong track record, and I'd done well in similar interviews before. By the end of the day, I was told I wouldn't be moving forward. Looking back, I learned more from that failure than from most of the interviews I'd passed.

The interview had four rounds: a behavioral conversation, a coding exercise, a system design discussion, and a final chat with the hiring manager. The behavioral round went smoothly. I had prepared STAR examples for common questions, and I felt I came across as thoughtful and genuine.

The coding round was where things started to slip. The interviewer gave me what looked like a standard problem — design a data structure that supports certain operations efficiently. I jumped in too quickly, wrote a solution, and then realized halfway through that my approach had a subtle performance issue. Instead of pausing, taking a breath, and rethinking, I tried to patch my solution on the fly. The result was messy code and a clearly uncertain author.

**Lesson 1:** When you realize your approach is wrong, stop and restart. Interviewers respect clarity far more than stubbornness. Saying "I think this approach won't work — let me try a different one" is a sign of maturity, not weakness.

System design was worse. The question was about designing a real-time notification system. The problem wasn't that I didn't know the technology; it was that I dove into details too early. I started talking about database choices and specific tools before I had even established the scale or the core requirements. The interviewer tried to steer me back with clarifying questions, but I kept racing ahead.

**Lesson 2:** In system design, the first five minutes are about asking questions, not answering them. Requirements, scale, and constraints come first. Technology comes last.

The final round was the killer. The hiring manager asked me, "Can you tell me about a time you received critical feedback and how you handled it?" I gave a polished answer about a minor stylistic disagreement from years earlier. The manager followed up with, "That sounds small — can you tell me about something more significant?" I froze. I hadn't prepared a real example, so I made one up on the spot. The manager could tell, and the energy of the conversation changed immediately.

**Lesson 3:** Interviewers can tell when you're reaching. Real examples — even messy ones — are more compelling than polished fiction. If you don't have an answer, it's better to say "I need a moment to think of one" than to invent something.

I didn't get the offer, and at the time it stung. But the rejection forced me to reflect on habits I hadn't noticed in myself — moving too fast, avoiding discomfort, and hiding behind prepared answers. The next time I interviewed, I did things differently: I slowed down, I asked more questions, and I gave honest answers even when they weren't flattering. I got that offer.

Failing taught me what passing never could.

## Story 2: The Question I Wasn't Expecting

When I was interviewing for my current job, the most memorable moment wasn't a hard technical problem — it was a single question I hadn't prepared for.

It was my third round, a conversation with one of the engineers I'd be working with if I got the offer. We'd been chatting for about twenty minutes about systems, debugging, on-call rotations — all the things you'd expect. Then she leaned back and asked: "What's something you believe about software engineering that most of your peers would disagree with?"

I genuinely didn't have an answer ready. I fumbled for a moment, then said the first honest thing that came to mind: "I think we write too many unit tests and not enough integration tests."

She raised an eyebrow. "Really? Why?"

I started explaining. Most unit tests, I said, end up testing the implementation details of the code — which means when you refactor, the tests break even though the behavior hasn't changed. This creates a tax on refactoring, which over time makes codebases more rigid, not less. Integration tests, by contrast, test behavior from the outside. They survive refactors, and they catch the interactions between components that unit tests miss. In my experience, teams that invest more in integration tests end up with codebases that are easier to change.

She pushed back — she had a different view, and we debated it for ten minutes. I didn't convince her, and she didn't convince me. But when I left that interview, I felt good about it in a way I hadn't expected. Not because I'd "won" the debate, but because the conversation had been **real** — two engineers with real opinions, disagreeing thoughtfully.

Later, I learned that was the moment she'd decided to hire me. In her words: "Anyone can recite best practices. I wanted someone who had formed their own views and could defend them without being defensive."

**The lesson here is surprisingly simple.** Interviews are not exams. The strongest candidates aren't the ones who know every right answer — they're the ones who can think clearly in real time, hold opinions they've actually earned, and defend those opinions without becoming rigid.

Before your next interview, ask yourself: **What do I actually believe about my craft?** Not what your manager believes, or what the textbook says, or what's popular on Twitter — what you, after years of experience, have come to think is true. If you can't answer that question in five minutes, spend some time on it. It will shape your interviews in ways you might not expect.

## Story 3: Lessons from Hiring

After being on the hiring side for two years and interviewing over a hundred engineers, I want to share what I've noticed about what makes someone memorable — in a good way.

First, the best candidates are almost always **curious**. When I describe a system, they ask questions about it. When I mention a problem we're solving, they want to know more. Weaker candidates, by contrast, tend to treat the interview as a one-way street — they answer when asked, but they don't engage. This isn't a minor thing. Curiosity signals that someone will be a good teammate, will keep learning, and will push back when the team is heading in the wrong direction. It's probably the single trait I value most.

Second, strong candidates are **honest about what they don't know**. This one surprised me when I first started hiring. I used to think that an uncertain answer would hurt a candidate's chances. It's the opposite. When someone says, "I haven't worked with that, but here's how I'd approach it," my confidence in them goes up. When someone pretends to know something they clearly don't, my confidence collapses. And interviewers can almost always tell the difference — we ask questions we know the answers to precisely so we can hear how candidates handle uncertainty.

Third, the strongest candidates can **tell a story about their work**. Not just "I built X" but "We were solving Y; we considered A and B; we chose A because of Z; here's what happened next." This kind of narrative shows that the candidate actually understood what they were doing, not just that they were present when it happened. It also shows judgment — the ability to see trade-offs and make reasonable decisions under uncertainty.

Fourth, and this is underrated: strong candidates are **easy to talk to**. They listen. They pick up on hints. If I say something like, "That approach has some risks at scale" and they keep going as if I hadn't spoken, they're not demonstrating the collaboration skills I'm looking for. The candidates I've hired all had a particular quality — they made conversations feel productive and warm, even when we disagreed.

What doesn't matter as much as you might think? Knowing every framework. Having the perfect resume. Solving the coding problem faster than everyone else. I've hired people who struggled with the coding exercise but clearly had the qualities above; they've almost always been among my best hires.

If you're preparing for interviews, my honest advice would be: stop trying to seem impressive, and start trying to be the kind of engineer you'd want to work with. Interviewers are mostly asking themselves one question: **Would I want this person on my team?** Everything else is secondary.
