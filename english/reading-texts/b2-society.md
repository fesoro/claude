## Text

Over the past decade, the way people work has changed dramatically. The so-called "gig economy" — a labour market characterised by short-term contracts and freelance work rather than permanent employment — has expanded rapidly, driven by digital platforms that connect workers with customers at the touch of a button. Companies like Uber, Deliveroo, and Fiverr have made it possible for millions of people to earn money on their own terms, choosing when and how much they work.

Proponents of the gig economy argue that it offers unparalleled flexibility. Workers can fit their jobs around their lives rather than the other way around. For students, parents, or those with multiple commitments, the ability to work irregular hours without answering to a traditional employer can be genuinely liberating. Many people also value the variety of gig work — the opportunity to try different roles, meet diverse clients, and develop a broad range of skills.

However, critics point to a darker side. Gig workers are typically classified as independent contractors rather than employees, which means they are not entitled to the same protections as regular workers. There is no sick pay, no paid holiday, no pension contributions from employers, and often no guaranteed minimum wage. A driver who cannot work due to illness, for example, simply does not earn. This creates a precarious existence for many who depend on gig platforms as their primary source of income.

The legal status of gig workers has become a major point of contention in many countries. In the United Kingdom, a landmark Supreme Court ruling in 2021 determined that Uber drivers must be classified as "workers" — a category between employee and self-employed — entitling them to certain rights, including minimum wage and holiday pay. This decision was seen as a significant step forward by workers' rights campaigners, though others warned it could lead to higher prices for consumers or reduced opportunities for drivers.

The debate ultimately reflects broader questions about what kind of society we want to live in. Should the economy prioritise flexibility and entrepreneurial freedom, or security and collective protection? The answer may depend on who is asking — and whether they are sitting in the back of the car, or behind the wheel.
