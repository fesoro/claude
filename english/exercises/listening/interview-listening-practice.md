# Interview Listening Practice

Bu məşqlər intervyu zamanı müsahibin suallarını dəqiq başa düşməyinizə kömək edir. Hər track fərqli intervyu növünü simulyasiya edir. Mətni yalnız bir dəfə oxuyun, sonra cavablayın.

## Track 1 — Phone Screening (A2/B1)
**Topic:** Initial HR phone call

**Recruiter:** Hi, is this Orkhan?

**Candidate:** Yes, speaking.

**Recruiter:** Great. This is Jennifer from TechGlobal. Thanks for applying for the Backend Developer position. I just wanted to have a quick chat — do you have about fifteen minutes?

**Candidate:** Yes, of course. Thank you for calling.

**Recruiter:** Perfect. So first, could you give me a brief overview of your current role and what you're doing day-to-day?

**Candidate:** Sure. I'm currently working as a developer at a software company. I mainly work on building APIs and maintaining our database systems. On a typical day, I write code, review pull requests, and attend team meetings.

**Recruiter:** That sounds relevant. And what's motivating you to look for a new opportunity right now?

**Candidate:** I've been in my current position for about two years now, and I feel like I've grown a lot, but I'm looking for a bigger challenge. I'm particularly interested in working with a more international team.

**Recruiter:** Makes sense. Now, this role requires experience with Python and PostgreSQL. How would you rate your skills in those areas?

**Candidate:** I'd say I'm quite comfortable with Python — I've been using it for about three years. PostgreSQL — I've used it on two major projects, so I'm familiar with it but I wouldn't call myself an expert yet.

**Recruiter:** That's fine — we don't expect everyone to be an expert on day one. One more thing — this position is remote, but we do require overlap with Central European Time for at least five hours per day. Would that work for you?

**Candidate:** Yes, absolutely. I'm currently in a time zone that's only two hours ahead, so that wouldn't be a problem at all.

**Recruiter:** Excellent. Last question — what are your salary expectations for this role?

**Candidate:** I've done some research and based on the market rate and my experience, I'd be looking at something in the range of fifty to sixty thousand euros annually. But I'm open to discussion.

**Recruiter:** That's within our range. OK, I think we have a good fit here. I'd like to move you forward to a technical interview. I'll send you an email with the details and available time slots. Does that sound good?

**Candidate:** That sounds great. Thank you very much, Jennifer.

**Recruiter:** Thank you, Orkhan. Talk to you soon. Bye!

### Questions — Track 1

**A. Complete the information. Write NO MORE THAN THREE WORDS AND/OR A NUMBER.**

1. The position is for a __________ Developer.
2. The call is expected to take about __________ minutes.
3. The candidate mainly builds __________ in his current role.
4. He has been in his current position for about __________.
5. He has been using Python for about __________.

**B. Choose the correct answer (A, B, or C).**

6. Why is the candidate looking for a new job?
   - A. He doesn't like his current colleagues
   - B. He wants a bigger challenge and an international team
   - C. His current company is closing

7. How does the candidate describe his PostgreSQL skills?
   - A. Expert level
   - B. Familiar but not expert
   - C. Complete beginner

8. What is the time zone requirement?
   - A. Must work US hours
   - B. At least 5 hours overlap with CET
   - C. Must be available 24/7

9. What is the candidate's salary expectation?
   - A. 40-50K euros
   - B. 50-60K euros
   - C. 60-70K euros

10. What is the next step?
    - A. An offer letter
    - B. A technical interview
    - C. A second phone screening

## Track 2 — Behavioral Interview (B1)
**Topic:** Competency-based questions

**Interviewer:** Thanks for joining us today. I'd like to ask you some questions about how you've handled situations in the past. Let's start with teamwork. Can you tell me about a time when you had a disagreement with a colleague? How did you handle it?

**Candidate:** Sure. In my previous role, I was working on a feature with another developer. We disagreed about the approach — I wanted to use a microservice architecture, and he preferred a monolithic approach. Instead of pushing my opinion, I suggested we both write a short document listing the pros and cons of each approach. We then discussed it together with the tech lead. In the end, we went with a hybrid approach that took the best parts of both ideas. It actually turned out better than either of our original plans.

**Interviewer:** Good example. Now, can you describe a situation where you had to deal with a tight deadline?

**Candidate:** Yes. About six months ago, our client moved the deadline for a major release forward by two weeks. We had a lot of features still in progress. I sat down with the team and we prioritised the features based on business impact. We decided to postpone three non-critical features and focus all our effort on the core functionality. I also suggested we do daily check-ins instead of our usual twice-a-week meetings. We delivered on time, and the client was happy with the result.

**Interviewer:** And how did you manage the pressure personally?

**Candidate:** To be honest, it was stressful. I made sure to communicate openly with the team — if something was taking longer than expected, I'd flag it immediately rather than trying to solve everything alone. I also tried to maintain a healthy routine — I didn't want to burn out, so I still took short breaks and kept reasonable working hours.

**Interviewer:** That's mature. Last question — tell me about a mistake you made at work and what you learned from it.

**Candidate:** Early in my career, I deployed a change to production without running the full test suite. It caused a bug that affected about two hundred users for an hour before we noticed and rolled it back. I felt terrible about it. After that, I became very careful about following the deployment checklist and I actually helped create an automated pre-deployment testing pipeline. Since then, we haven't had a similar incident. The lesson for me was that shortcuts in development almost always cost more time in the long run.

### Questions — Track 2

4. How much earlier was the client's deadline moved?
5. How often did the team have check-ins during the crunch?
