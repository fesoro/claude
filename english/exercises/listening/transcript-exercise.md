## Track 1 — IELTS Style: Section 1
**Topic:** Booking a language course

*[Phone rings]*

**Receptionist:** Good morning, City Language Centre. How can I help you?

**Caller:** Hello. I'm calling because I'd like to enrol on one of your English courses. I saw your advertisement in the local newspaper.

**Receptionist:** Of course! Could I take a few details first? What's your name, please?

**Caller:** Yes, it's Maria — M-A-R-I-A — Fernandez. F-E-R-N-A-N-D-E-Z.

**Receptionist:** Thank you, Maria. And could I ask what your current level of English is?

**Caller:** Well, I'm not sure exactly. I've been learning for about six years, so I think maybe intermediate?

**Receptionist:** We'd recommend taking our placement test first, just to be sure. It only takes about twenty minutes and it's completely free. When would you be available?

**Caller:** I'm free on Tuesday and Thursday mornings, and Friday afternoons.

**Receptionist:** Let me check... we have a slot on Thursday the 14th at 10 a.m. Would that suit you?

**Caller:** Thursday the 14th at ten? Yes, that's perfect.

**Receptionist:** Excellent. Now, in terms of courses, are you interested in general English or do you have a specific goal, like exam preparation?

**Caller:** Actually, I'm hoping to take the IELTS exam next year. I need it for a university application.

**Receptionist:** In that case, I'd suggest our IELTS Preparation course. It runs every Monday, Wednesday, and Friday from 6 to 8 p.m. The next intake starts on the 3rd of March.

**Caller:** How much does it cost?

**Receptionist:** The course is twelve weeks long and the fee is £480 in total. However, if you pay in full before the course starts, you get a 10% discount.

**Caller:** That's £432 then? That sounds reasonable. And is there anything I need to bring to the placement test?

**Receptionist:** Just a form of ID — a passport or driving licence — and a pen. We'll provide everything else.

**Caller:** Great. Could you give me the address?

**Receptionist:** Of course. We're at 47 Bridge Street, that's B-R-I-D-G-E Street, in the city centre. We're opposite the main post office.

**Caller:** Brilliant. Thank you very much.

**Receptionist:** You're welcome. See you on Thursday!

## Track 2 — General Listening: Monologue
**Topic:** A radio talk about urban gardening

*[Radio programme intro music fades]*

**Presenter:** Welcome back to Green Futures. Today I want to talk about something that's quietly transforming cities around the world — urban gardening. More and more city dwellers are turning unused rooftops, balconies, and even walls into productive growing spaces. But why is this happening now, and what are the real benefits?

The most obvious reason is access to fresh food. In many urban areas — particularly lower-income neighbourhoods — fresh vegetables and fruit are either expensive or difficult to find. Community gardens provide residents with affordable, locally grown produce and, in many cases, bring neighbours together in the process.

But the benefits go beyond food. Studies have shown that gardening reduces stress, lowers blood pressure, and improves mental health. The act of nurturing something living — watching seeds germinate and plants grow — has a deeply calming effect that many urban residents find increasingly hard to access.

There's also an environmental argument. Urban gardens absorb carbon dioxide, reduce the urban heat island effect, and support insect populations, particularly bees and other pollinators that are critical to food security. Even a small rooftop garden can make a measurable difference to local biodiversity.

Of course, there are challenges. Space is limited, soil quality can be poor, and growing food in cities requires time and knowledge that not everyone has. Water use is also a concern in drought-prone regions. But new technologies — hydroponics, vertical farming, and automated irrigation systems — are making urban gardening more accessible and efficient than ever before.

The future, I think, is a city where food production is woven into the very fabric of urban life — where the journey from seed to plate is measured in metres, not miles.

## Questions — Monologue

**Questions 1–4: Choose the correct letter A, B, or C.**

1. According to the presenter, urban gardening is becoming more popular because:
   - A. it is cheaper than buying food from supermarkets
   - B. it is transforming how people think about city living
   - C. governments are actively encouraging it

2. Community gardens are said to be particularly beneficial in:
   - A. wealthy areas with large open spaces
   - B. areas where fresh food is hard to access
   - C. neighbourhoods with high crime rates

3. The presenter mentions that gardening can:
   - A. cure certain medical conditions
   - B. reduce stress and improve mental health
   - C. replace the need for exercise

4. Which of the following is described as a challenge for urban gardening?
   - A. Lack of public interest
   - B. Government restrictions
   - C. Limited space and poor soil quality

**Questions 5–8: True / False / Not Given**

5. Urban gardens have no measurable effect on local biodiversity.
6. Water consumption is a concern in some regions.
7. Hydroponics is described as a recently invented technology.
8. The presenter believes food production will eventually become part of city design.
